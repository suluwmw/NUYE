Preparatory | Chamber | Winds | Symphony
Shiver Randall D. Standridge

"Shiver" is a great autumnal song with imagery of a cold night. The students learn how to tremolo and play back and forth with the melodies.

Nightmare On the Housetop Traditional arr. John M. Pasternak

This piece is a take on the traditional piece "Up On The Housetop." The composer says that children are excited for Santa to come but then they are surprised with Halloween characters instead. This piece goes back and forth between bow (arco) and plucking (pizzicato).

Into the Midnight Forest Mekel Rogers

"Into the Midnight Forest" has a mysterious-march-through-the-darkness feel. It's a fun piece that brings in great dynamics and a lot of staccato (short) bowings. It also brings in a few legato (smooth) bowings as well for contrast.

Dance of the Tumblers from Snow Maiden Nikolai Rimsky-Korsakov arr. Sandra Dackow

This piece, Dance of the Tumblers, accompanies the energetic dancing of Russian travelling entertainers as the villagers wait for a contest to see who will win Snow Maiden's heart.

Midnight Storm Erica Donahoe

In this contemporary piece, dramatic eighth-note phrases evoke the experience of a storm blowing in after dark.

Shackelford Banks Jay Bocook

Named for the uninhabited island off the North Carolina coast where wild horses still roam, this energetic original features a distinctive western flavor. The tough "Banker Ponies" are descended from Spanish horses that probably swam to shore after shipwrecks in the 1500s. They eat dune grass and dig shallow pits to reach fresh water.

March of the Shadows Haley Woodrow

Paying homage to the traditional march form, this work steps outside the box in this delightfully dark and unique piece in a minor key.

Pure Imagination Leslie Bricusse & Anthony Newley arr. Paul Murtha

Originally sung by Gene Wilder in the 1971 movie classic Willy Wonka & the Chocolate Factory, this timeless song has remained a favorite ever since. This setting is based on the version sung by Timothée Chalamet for the movie Wonka and features lush harmonies and an appealing pop groove. Magical!

Today, NUYS presents four Romantic works.
The Romantics overturned the formality and order of the Classical Era with their emotion-laden music. Romantics focused on the individual, the mystical, and the unusual. Today's composers were each influential in their own way: Beethoven ushered in the era, Berlioz continued it with a passion, Accolay built violin skills, and Bizet added a dash of realism near the end.

Egmont Overture

Ludwig van Beethoven
(BAY-toh-ven)
The Egmont Overture has been called the "distilled essence of Beethoven." Written during his middle period, it still relies on classical structure but adds the narrative arc and powerful emotions of Romanticism.

Violin Concerto No. 1 in A minor

Jean-Baptiste Accolay
(AH-coh-lay)
The liner notes from famous violinist Itzhak Perlman's album Concertos from my Childhood refer to Accolay as "long-forgotten." But this piece hasn't been. It's a one-movement gem of lyric romanticism that showcases a violinist's phrasing and expression.

"Hungarian March" from The Damnation of Faust

Hector Berlioz
(BEHR-lee-ohz)
This dramatic and exuberent march occurs in Act I of Berlioz's The Damnation of Faust. It's full of vitality and energy, in contrast to the title character. Berlioz based the march on a Hungarian melody, and when it was first played in that country during one of his conducting tours, audiences cheered so loudly they drowned out the music.

Carmen Suite No. 1

Georges Bizet
(bee-ZAY)
Carmen was a shock to French opera-goers. They were perfectly used to the melodrama, but they weren't used to seeing the actual murder acted onstage. And the heroine wasn't conventional - she was a working-class woman who did as she pleased.
